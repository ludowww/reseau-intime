# Human Dialogue Review Guide

## Purpose
This guide explains how to review SMS dialogue drafts after they pass technical QA.

## Technical QA vs human review
Technical QA checks structure, speakers, choices, intensity, and basic consistency. Human review checks whether the scene feels alive, specific, emotionally credible, and non-generic. A draft can pass technical QA and still need editorial revision.

## How to review a draft
1. Run technical QA.
2. Read only the messages first, without metadata.
3. Ask whether the speakers are identifiable without names.
4. Check whether Player feels present.
5. Check whether the scene begins from a concrete trigger.
6. Check whether the tension is shown through behavior rather than explained.
7. Score the rubric.
8. Write at most 3 revision notes.

## Scoring dimensions
- Natural SMS rhythm: the exchange should feel like private texting, not polished literary dialogue.
- Character-specific voice: each speaker should remain identifiable without their name.
- Player presence: Player should read as a believable person, not just a response machine.
- Relationship proximity: the scene should show how long and how deeply the characters know each other.
- Concrete trigger: the conversation should begin from a believable reason to text.
- Subtext and denial: tension should stay readable without being over-explained.
- Choice quality: choices should be actual SMS that could be sent.
- Memory continuity feel: memory should feel lived rather than mechanically referenced.
- Desire context: desire should be rooted in the relationship and situation.
- Anti-AI texture: the scene should avoid balanced, over-clean, over-explained dialogue.
- Route coherence: the scene should respect the route identity and current phase.
- Cross-route awareness: active routes should color each other without caricature.

## Automatic rejection flags
- Choices are not sendable SMS.
- Player is absent or purely functional for most of the scene.
- Voices are interchangeable.
- The scene explains psychology instead of dramatizing it.
- A Day 1 or Day 2 scene forces confession, crisis, or route lock too early.
- Marie is reduced to guilt or suspicion.
- Sandra is reduced to forbidden temptation.
- Generic flirtation replaces character-specific behavior.

## What “too AI” usually looks like
Everyone answers too perfectly. Every message is clever. The rhythm is too symmetrical. Characters explain the subtext. The scene has no mundane anchor. The emotional progression is too clean. The same sentence structure repeats. The dialogue sounds like a therapy summary.

## Character-specific reminders
### Sandra
Sandra should feel like a returning complicity, not a generic temptation. She can be active, but not forceful. Her ambiguity should remain deniable early. She can test whether Player also felt the distance.

### Marie
Marie should feel like present-life intimacy, not guilt. She can notice presence quality before facts. She should remain desirable through the everyday. She should not become suspicious or punitive too early.

### Player
Player should remain discreet, observant, slightly ironic when embarrassed, maladroit but sincere. He can be divided without becoming cynical or cruel. His choices must be real SMS.

## Examples of useful revision notes
- Replace the abstract opening with a concrete trigger.
- Make Sandra dodge with humor instead of explaining her hesitation.
- Give Marie one ordinary domestic detail before the emotional line.
- Make Player repair through a message he could actually send.
- Remove one clever exchange and add a small imperfect pause.

## When to mark a draft as reference quality
A draft can be marked as reference quality when it passes technical QA, has no rejection flag, most dimensions score 3 or higher, and the character voice would still be recognizable without speaker labels.
